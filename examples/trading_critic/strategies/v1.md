# Strategy v1 — Moving-average crossover

## Premise

Long-only directional strategy on a single liquid equity index ETF (SPY).
Entry on a fast moving average crossing above a slow moving average, exit on
the reverse cross. No other components.

## Parameters

- Fast MA: 20-day simple moving average of close.
- Slow MA: 50-day simple moving average of close.
- Position sizing: 100% of available capital per trade. No fractional sizing.
- Stop-loss: none. Trailing stop: none. Profit target: none.
- Holding period: variable, defined entirely by the next crossover signal.
- Universe: SPY only. No diversification across assets.

## Entry / exit logic

```
if fast_ma_today > slow_ma_today and fast_ma_yesterday <= slow_ma_yesterday:
    open_long_position(allocation=available_cash)
elif fast_ma_today < slow_ma_today and fast_ma_yesterday >= slow_ma_yesterday:
    close_long_position()
```

Signal evaluated on close-to-close daily bars. Orders submitted at the open
of the following session at market.

## Risk management

The crossover signal itself is the only risk control. The author's reasoning:
"if the trend turns, the crossover will exit me before the drawdown gets
serious." There is no separate stop, no volatility-based sizing, no
maximum-drawdown circuit-breaker, and no regime filter.

The author also notes that during 2020-03 the strategy would have stayed
long through the COVID drawdown until the 20/50 cross occurred, which on
SPY happened on 2020-03-09. They consider this acceptable because the
strategy "made it back."

## What the author wants the critic to evaluate

How robust is this approach across market regimes? Is the absence of
explicit risk controls a meaningful gap, given that the crossover provides
a directional exit? Is there novelty here, or is this a textbook approach?
